# Spanish Borrowings in New Mexico English: Statistical Analysis


## Overview
The history and presence of the Spanish language is deeply embedded in the state of New Mexico, and as of 2012 47% of New Mexicans self-identified as Hispanic/Latino (Pew Hispanic Center, 2012). While there are numerous studies on the influence of English in New Mexico Spanish (Bills, 1997; Bills and Vigil, 2008), there are no studies that consider the effects of Spanish on English in the region. While contact effects are typically fewer for the dominant language, the length of contact between these languages makes New Mexico a unique context to study Spanish lexical influence on English. Thus, considering only the English lexicon of New Mexicans, the main objective of this study is to assess how age, gender, ethnicity, generational ties to New Mexico, and exposure to Spanish, affect participants’ usage of thirty-seven Spanish borrowings, such as mitotero and órale, for example. 

## Research Question

- How do age, gender, ethnicity, generational ties to New Mexico, and exposure to Spanish affect usage of Spanish borrowings in English? 

## Methodology
- Large-scale online survey
- Survey was online for 3 months and total participants N=202
- 37 Borrowings were included in this survey
- Based on the goals of this study, the independent variables analyzed:
  1.	Generation: if a participant has first-, second-, or third-generation New Mexican heritage
  2.	Heritage Speaker: if Spanish was reported as used in the household
  3.	Ethnicity: a self-reporting of ethnic categories used by the US Census
  4.	Age: a self-reporting of a participant’s age group (18-24, 25-34, 35-44, 45-54, 55-64, 65-74, or 75+)
  5.	Gender: male or female

To determine the best predictors of use of Spanish borrowings, I conducted analysis of variance (ANOVA) tests on each independent variable. If ANOVA tests demonstrated significant results, then I incorporated the significant variables into a linear regression model. Since more than one independent variable was found to be significant, multiple linear regression models were created. The Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) values for multiple linear regression models were then compared and the best model had the lowest AIC and the highest r-squared values.  Two-sample t-tests were used to compare the average percent of use of the Spanish borrowings within and across independent variables. The results of these analyses are presented in the next section. 


## Key Findings

- Generational heritage to New Mexico = statistically significant predictor 
- Self-identified ethnicity = statistically significant predictor 
- 37 Spanish borrowings analyzed
